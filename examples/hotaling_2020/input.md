# Writing Concisely in the Sciences

> A demonstration passage written for praxis in the spirit of Hotaling (2020),
> "Simple rules for concise scientific writing", Limnology and Oceanography
> Letters 5:379-383, https://doi.org/10.1002/lol2.10165. It exercises the
> verbose patterns the paper targets; it is not quoted from the paper.

It should be noted that concise writing is not a stylistic luxury but a scientific obligation. Readers have the ability to absorb only so much prose in a sitting, and every needless word competes with the evidence for their attention. It is important to note that reviewers report fatigue as a leading cause of superficial reviews (Hotaling, 2020).

Authors often add hedging in order to sound rigorous. In practice, the opposite occurs due to the fact that hedged claims are harder to test. When we perform an analysis of a manuscript's length, roughly 25% of its words can usually be removed with no loss of meaning, a figure consistent with editing exercises reported across writing guides [1].

A writer who wants to conduct an evaluation of their own drafts can start small: cut introductory throat-clearing, replace nominalizations with verbs, and prefer short sentences, because a sentence that stretches across many clauses in order to qualify every possible objection before finally arriving at its main verb demands far more from the reader than the same content delivered as two or three plain statements would ever require.

Concision is a habit. Edit ruthlessly, measure the result, and let the 25% rule keep you honest.
